Decision Analysis

The conservative and optimistic methods

Consider the following payoff table for several decisions. D1-4 represents several decisions that we could make, while low-high represent the current state of the economy. The numbers represent possible profits.

Low Medium High
D1 50 80 105
D2 70 85 90
D3 20 70 150
D4 20 60 140

The conservative approach chooses the highest possible outcome out of the worst outcomes. When the economy is low, the highest profits are given by decision D2.

The optimistic approach chooses the highest possible outcome available. In this case, that outcome is given by choice D3.

The Regret Approach

The regret approach chooses the option with lowest maximum regret. Regret is defined as the difference between the best decision and your decision given the variable. In this case, the difference given the state of the economy.

Regret can be shown with a regret matrix:

Low Medium High
D1 70 - 50 85 - 80 150 - 105
D2 70 - 70 85 - 85 150 - 90
D3 70 - 20 85 - 70 150 - 50
D4 70 - 20 85 - 60 150 - 140

The selected boxes repesent the values where regret is maximum. The maximum regret from each decision would then be:

Regret
D1 45
D2 60
D3 50
D4 50

Since decision D1 has the lowest regret out of the other decisions, D1 would be the preferred choice.

Expected Monetary Value

Say we now have the information of the probabilities of the economy being low, medium, or high. The probabilities are as follows:

Low Medium High
P() 0.5 0.2 0.3

We can then use the expected monetary value method to help make decisions. We essentially calculate the expected value from each decision and choose the decision with the highest expected profits:

Expected Monetary Value
D1 0.5(50) + 0.2(80) + 0.3(105) = 72.5
D2 0.5(70) + 0.2(85) + 0.3(90) = 79
D3 0.5(20) + 0.2(70) + 0.3(150) = 69
D4 0.5(20) + 0.2(60) + 0.3(140) = 64

D2 has the highest expected value, thus D2 is the optimal decision.

Value of Information

Say we have the option to receive perfect information about our decisions. That is, we know for sure if the economy would be at a low, medium, or high condition. How much are we willing to pay for this information?

The value of information would be the difference between our expected profit with perfect information and our expected profit without:

EVPI = EPPI - EMV

If we know the state of the economy, then our decision would be simple. We would just choose the decision with the highest output given the economic state.

If Choose Profits
Low D2 70
Medium D2 85
High D3 150

Given the above probabilities, the expected profits with perfect information would be:

E(X) = 70(0.5) + 85(0.2) + 150(0.3) = 97

We know that the EMV is 79 (from the table above), and so we would pay up to 97 - 79 = 18 for perfect information.
