Budgeting

The Sales Budget

Total budgeted sales is simply the total amount of sales that are expected in a given fiscal period. It is therefore as simple as:

Budgeted sales = expected # units sold x unit selling price

Expected Cash Collections

The expected cash collections may not necessarily match the budgeted sales, as some sales may be done on account.

To find the expected cash collections, we need to know the company's collection pattern. That is, how much and when a company expects to collect their sales on account.

Example Problem

The Royal Company expects the following sales for the quarter ending June 30. All sales are on account, and the March 31 accounts receivable balance of $30,000 will be collected in full.

April May June
$200,000 $500,000 $300,000

Royal's collection pattern is as follows:

  • 70% collected in the month of sale
  • 25% collected in the month following sale
  • 5% uncollectible

What are the total cash collections for June?

Show Solution

Since we know Royal's collection pattern, this is quite simple. Since 5% is uncollectible, we disregard 5% of June's sales.

Then, we simply add up 70% of this month's sales with 25% of last month's sales:

300,000(0.7) + 500,000(0.25) = $335,000

The Production Budget

The production budget must account for adequate production such that it satisfies budgeted sales and sufficient ending inventory.

For example, if Royal company wishes that:

  • Ending inventory is equal to 20% of the following month's budgeted sales in units.
  • Ending inventory at end of quarter is 5,000 units.

At the end of March 4,000 units were at hand, and unit selling price is equal to $10.

What is the required production for May?

Show Solution

If the unit selling price is $10 a unit, then the budgeted sales for April and May are 20,000 and 50,000 units respectively.

For May, the beginning inventory is the desired ending for April, which is 20% of May's sales, which is 0.2 x 50,000 = 10,000 units.

The desired ending inventory is 20% of June's sales, which is 0.2 x 30,000 = 6,000 units.

Therefore the required production of May is:

The Direct Materials Budget

The direct materials budget details the raw materials that must be purchased to fulfill the production budget and to provide for adequate inventories.

For Royal company, consider the following information:

  • Five pounds of material are required per unit of product.
  • Management requires that materials on hand at the end of each month are equal to 10% of the next month's production.
  • On March 31st, 13,000 pounds of material are on hand.

How much materials should be purchased in May?

Show Solution

The beginning inventory for May is 10% of May's material needs, which is 10% of 230,000, 23,000. The ending inventory is 10% of June's material needs, which is 10% x 29,000 x 5 = 14,500

Therefore the materials to be purchased for May is:

Expected-Cash Disembursement

It is also important to know how much money is needed to pay for the materials that we need. This entirely depends on how the company chooses to pay for the materials.

Consider the following information for Royal company:

  • Royal pays $0.40 per pound for its materials.
  • One-half of a month's purchase is paid during the month. The other half is paid in the following month.

What would the expected cash disembursement be during the month of May?

Show Solution

The cash outflow during the month of May is half for paying the materials purchased during May, and half for paying the materials purchased in April. Therefore:

Cost materials in May = 0.4 x 221,500 = $88,600
Cost materials in April = 0.4 x 140,000 = $56,000

Total cash outflow in May = 0.5(88,600) + 0.5(56,000) = $72,300

  • The cash and bank credit card sale payments are received in the month of sale.
  • Bank credit card sales are subject to a 4% discount, which is deducted immediately.
  • Cash receipts for sales on open account are 70% in the month following the sale and 28% in the second month following the sale.

TabComp's month end inventory requirements for computer hardware are 30% of the next month's sales. The units must be ordered two months in advance due to long lead times from the supplier.

Calculate the cash that TabComp can expect to collect in April.

Show Solution

The cash that will be collected can be split from three different sources: cash sales, credit card sales, and account sales.

Cash sales are simply 25% of April's total sales:

Cash sales =
0.25 x $400,000 = $100,000

Credit card sales are 30% of total sales with a 4% discount applied. Therefore:

Credit card sales =
0.96 x (0.30 x $400,000) = 0.96 x $120,000 = $115,200

For account sales, we have to consider the total sales of March and February. In April, we will collect:

  • 70% of March's account sales.
  • 28% of February's account sales.

March collection =
0.70 x (0.45 x $480,000) = $151,200

February collection =
0.28 x (0.45 x $500,000) = $63,000

Total account sales = 151,200 + 63,000 = $214,200

Total cash collections in April is the sum of all cash, credit, and account collections, which equals to $429,400.

Determine the number of computer hardware units that should be ordered in January.

Show Solution

In January we have to order for the units that are required in March, because of the time restrictions mentioned in the problem.

The beginning inventory in March would be 30% of March's sales. The ending inventory in March would be 30% of April's sales.

Beginning inventory = 0.30 x 110 = 33 units
Ending inventory = 0.30 x 90 = 27 units

Flexible Budgets

The budgets that we have been working with so far are static. That is, they are prepared for a single fixed level of activity.

In reality it is very much possible for the true level of activity to differ from the budgeted level of activity. It is unreasonable to compare costs if the level of activity differs.

Flexible budgets can be prepared to any level of activity. To prepare a flexible budget, we need the variable cost per unit of activity and total fixed costs.

Example Problem

Say we are asked to construct a flexible manufacturing overhead budget. We are given the following per unit variable costs:

Indirect labour 4.00
Indirect material 3.00
Indirect power 0.50

And the following fixed costs:

Depreciation 12,000
Insurance 2,000

Construct a budget for 8,000 machine hours.

Making a flex budget is as simple as multiplying all of the unit costs by the machine hours we are given then adding the fixed costs:

Variances

With a flexible budget, we can more accurately compare budgeted and actual amounts. The difference between a budgeted amount and the actual amount is called a variance.

A favorable variance is when the actual amount is less than the budgeted amount.

An unfavorable variance is when the actual amount is more than the budgeted amount.
