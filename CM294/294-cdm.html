Relevant Costs For Decision Making

Adding/Dropping a Product Line or Segment

The general rule is that if the contribution margin lost is larger than the fixed cost loss and contribution margin gain, keep the segment:

Keep if: CM Lost > Fixed Expenses Avoided + CM Gain

There are two possible approaches: the contribution approach, and the income approach.

Contribution Approach

The contribution approach only looks at all the factors that change.

The segment should only be dropped only if its profits would increase afterwards. That is, only keep the segment if the total fixed cost avoidable exceeds the lost contribution margin.

Comparative Income

The comparative income approach prepares two income statements before and after the decision to drop the segment (assuming this decision is pushed forward).

Beware of Allocated Common Costs

Allocated common costs may make a segment look unprofitable even though dropping it may result in an increase in the overall company net income.

Allocated common costs that would not be affected by a decision are irrelevant and should be ignored.

The Make or Buy Decision

A decision to carry out one of the activities in the value chain internally rather than to buy from a supplier is called a make or buy decision.

Make Advantages:

  • Smoother flow of parts and materials for production.
  • Better quality control.
  • Profits realized from the parts & materials that they make instead of buy.

Buy Advantages:

  • Economies of scale of the suppliers.
  • Specialized suppliers may be able to respond more quickly and at less cost to changing needs.
  • Changing technology may make producing one's own parts riskier than purchasing from the outside.

To make a decision, we only look at costs. We choose to make only if:


Avoidable Cost To Make < Avoidable Cost To Buy

Opportunity Costs

An opportunity cost is the benefit foregone as a result of pursuing some other course of action.

Opportunity costs should be considered when making decisions. The telltale sign of an opportunity cost is when you have scarce resources or a constraint.

When there is excess capacity there is no opportunity cost.

Special Orders

A special-order is a one-time order that is not considered part of the company's normal ongoing business. The decision rule is:

Accept if: Incremental Revenue > Incremental Costs

If the special order requires a constrained resource, opportunity costs should be included as part of the incremental costs.

If a partial order may be accepted, then there would be no opportunity costs (even if there is a constrained resource).

Utilization of a Constrained Resource

When a limited resource of some type restricts the company's ability to satisfy demand, the company is said to have a constraint. To optimize the constraint, prioritize based on contribution margin per scarce resource.

Example

A furniture company only has 2,000 board feet this month. Data for the company is as follows:

Chairs Tables
Selling price per unit $80 $400
Variable cost per unit $30 $200
Board feet per unit 2 10
Monthly demand 600 100

How would you maximize profits?

Show Solution

The unit contribution margin for both products would be:

Chairs: $80 - $30 = $50
Tables: $400 - $200 = $200

Therefore the contribution margin per board foot would be:

Chairs: $50 / 2 = $25 per board foot
Tables: $200 / 10 = $20 per board foot

Since the chairs have the highest contribution margin per board foot, we should prioritize in producing chairs. We use all of our resources to satisfy the demand of the chairs, then use whatever is remaining to produce tables.

If the part was purchased from an outside supplier, all direct labour cost would be avoided. However, $5.10 of the fixed MOH would remain.

How much of the unit product cost is relevant in this decision?

Show Solution

All but $5.10 of the fixed MOH would be avoidable. Therefore out of the $56.70 unit product cost, 56.70 - 5.10 = $51.60 is relevant.

What is the net total dollar advantage (disadvantage) of purchasing the part rather than making it?

Show Solution

The total cost for producing the good would be:

56.70 x 20,000 = 1,134,000

The total product cost of buying the good instead would be:

Total purchase cost + unavoidable MOH =
(51.80 x 20,000) + (5.10 x 20,000) = 1,138,000

However we need to remember that, by buying the product instead, we can receive an additional $44,000. This is an opportunity cost.

Therefore the true cost of producing the good would be:

Manufacturing costs + opportunity costs =
1,134,000 + 44,000 = 1,178,000

The total dollar advantage would be:

1,178,000 - 1,138,000 = +40,000

What is the maximum per unit amount the company should be willing to pay an outside supplier?

Show Solution

We know that the true cost of manufacturing the good is 1,178,000.

At this amount, the company would be indifferent between purchasing or making the good. Thus, this is the maximum amount the company should pay.

In per unit terms:

1,178,000 / 20,000 = $58.90

An overseas customer has ordered 2,000 units at a discounted price. This order would have no effect on the company's normal sales and would not change the company's total fixed costs. The variable selling and administrative expenses would be $0.70 less per unit on this order than on normal sales.

Suppose there is ample idle capacity to produce the units required by the overseas customer, and the discounted price on the special order is $51.20 per unit.

How would this special order affect the company's net income?

Show Solution

Essentially we need to see if we will receive a net increase or decrease in income. That is, we need to find if the revenue we receive will be larger than or smaller than the costs.

Since there is excess capacity, there are no opportunity costs. Therefore the revenue we would receive is:

$51.20 x 2,000 units = 102,400

The total variable cost for this individual unit would be the sum of all VCs, which is $34.30. However we save some money on selling and admin, therefore the true cost would be 34.30 - 0.70 = $33.60.

Thus the total incremental cost would be:

$33.60 x 2,000 units = 67,200

The effect on net income is thus:

102,400 - 67,200 = +$35,200

Suppose the company is already operating at capacity when the special order is received from the overseas customer.

What would be the opportunity cost of each unit delivered to the overseas customer?

Show Solution

The opportunity cost would be equal to the lost contribution margin as a result of the lost sales:

OC = UCM lost = $56.70 - $34.30 = $22.40

Suppose there is not enough idle capacity to produce all of the units for the overseas customer, and accepting the special order would require cutting back on production of 700 units for regular customers.

What would be the minimum acceptable price per unit for the special order?

Show Solution

Here we use a similar concept to transfer pricing. The minimum price would be the variable cost plus all opportunity costs. Unlike the previous question, we are only displaying 700 units here instead of the entire 2000.

Minimum price =
Variable cost + (CM lost from sales)/(# of units in order) =
33.60 + (22.40 x 700)/(2000) = $41.44

Joint Costs

Often multiple end products are made from common inputs. Two or more products that are produced from a common input are called joint products.

The point in the manufacturing process where each joint product can be recognized as a separate product is called the split-off point.

Joint costs are irrelevant in decisions regarding what to do with the product from the split-off point forward.

It is always profitable to manufacture a joint product after the split off point as long as:

Incremental CM > 0
