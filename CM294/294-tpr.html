Transfer Pricing

A transfer price is the price charged when one segment of a company provides goods/services to another segment of the company. The "supplier" provides an intermediate product/service to the "buying" division.

The transfer price shifts profits in the company but does not (directly) affect the company as a whole.

There are three methods to determine the transfer price: market-based, cost-based, or negotiated.

Market-Based

Uses the market price of similar goods traded on an external market to set your internal transfer price.

Cost-Based

Transfer price is based on cost of producing the product. Usually the full (absorption) cost using standard costs is used, and some type of markup is added.

Negotiated

The selling division and buying division negotiate on a transfer price.

The highest acceptable price would be the cost of the product on the external market. The lowest acceptable price would be the cost per unit to produce + the opportunity cost of selling the product internally.

Essentially:

Minimum transfer price =
Variable cost + (Total CM lost / # units transferred)

When negotiating, there are three different scenarios to consider:

  1. Supplying division has excess capacity, i.e. transfer does not affect outside sales.
  2. Supplying division has no excess capacity.
  3. Supplying division has partial excess capacity.

Excess Capacity

If the supplier has excess capacity, then the transfer has no affect on outside sales. Thus, the minimum transfer price the supplier should accept would be:

Minimum transfer price = Variable cost

No Excess Capacity

If there is no excess capacity, then internal transfers come entirely at the expense of outside sales. The price of every unit transferred must also cover the opportunity cost. Therefore:

Minimum transfer price =
Variable cost +
(Lost CM from lost sales for entire # of units transferred) / # of units transferred

Partial Excess Capacity

If there is partial excess capacity, then internal transfers come partially at the expense of outside sales. Therefore:

Minimum transfer price =
Variable cost +
(Lost CM from lost sales only for # units of lost sales) / # of units transferred

Example 1: Box Company

The cardboard division wants to purchase 480,000 pounds of pulp from the pulp division. The pulp division has ample capacity, and the cardboard division can buy pulp on the market for $13 per pound.

Management uses a cost-plus transfer price for the pulp division, with a markup of 10%.

Total cost per unit (variable and fixed) is $12.90. Fixed cost per unit is $1.05.

Would the cardboard division accept the transfer price?

The cardboard division would not accept the transfer price.

Using a cost-plus transfer price, the transfer price would be:

12.90 x 1.10 = $14.19

However, the cardboard division is able to get the pulp in the external market for only $13. So they will not accept the transfer price.

How could an acceptable transfer price be determined?

The cardboard and pulp divisions would have to negotiate a transfer price.

Since the pulp division has ample (excess) capacity, the lowest acceptable would just be the variable cost of producing the pulp:

12.90 - 1.05 = $11.85

The highest acceptable would be the price in the external market, which is $13. Therefore the transfer price would be:

11.85 < transfer price < 13

Example 2: Barker Company

Barker company has two division - Batteries and Vehicles. The vehicles divison needs to purchase 50,000 units of batteries per year. It is currently buying the batteries from an external supplier for $39 per battery. More information:

  • The battery division has a production capacity of 300,000 units.
  • It can sell batteries on the market for $40 per unit.
  • Variable costs per battery are $18, and fixed costs per battery (based on capacity) are $7.

Suppose the Battery division has enough idle capacity to supply the Vehicle division without diverting batteries from the outside market.

What is the lowest acceptable transfer price from the viewpoint of the selling division?

Show Solution

Since there is enough idle capacity, i.e. the Battery Division has excess capacity, then the minimum transfer price is just variable cost - $18.

Suppose the Battery Division is already operating at capacity selling to the outside market.

What is the lowest acceptable transfer price from the viewpoint of the selling division?

Show Solution

Since the Battery Division is operating at full capacity, then transfers would come completely at the cost of sales. Therefore:

Minimum transfer price =
VC + Lost CM = 18 + (40-18) = $40

Suppose the Battery Division is still operating at capacity, however it can avoid $4 of variable costs for internal sales.

What is the lowest acceptable transfer price from the viewpoint of the selling division?

Show Solution

The reduction in VC would result in a lower overall transfer price. However, since it is still operating at capacity, lost CM still needs to be considered:

Minimum transfer price =
VC + Lost CM = (18-4) + (40-18) = $36

Now the Vehicle Division wants the Battery Division to supply it with 20,000 special heavy-duty batteries.

  • The variable cost for each heavy-duty battery would be $27.
  • The Battery Division has no idle capacity.
  • Heavy-duty batteries require more processing time, and so will displace 22,000 regular batteries from production.

What is the minimum transfer price for the heavy-duty batteries?

Show Solution

The heavy-duty batteries will display 22,000 units of regular batteries. This means that the # of units transferred is not equal to the # of units of lost sales. Therefore:

Minimum transfer price =
VC + (lost CM for sales lost)/(# of units transferred) =
27 + ((40-18) x 22,000)/20,000 = 27 + 24.2 = 51.2
