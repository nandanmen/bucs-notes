Variable & Absorption Costing

Variable vs. Absorption

Variable costing, also called direct costing or marginal costing, is when product costs only refer to variable manufacturing costs.

Absorption costing, also called full costing, is the financial-accounting costing system. It treats all manufacturing costs, including fixed costs, as product costs.

Selling & admin expenses are generally treated as a period cost under both costing systems.

Note how, under absorption costing, net operating income is $30,000 higher. The reason behind this is quite subtle.

The reason for this is that, essentially, fixed MOH is accrued in inventory. This results in the value of inventory being much higher in absorption costing than in variable.

Under absorption, COGM is $400,000 whereas under variable, it is $250,000.

Ending inventory is subsequently a higher value under absorption than under variable. In fact, it is exactly $30,000 higher. Effectively only $120,000 of fixed MOH was expensed in the year, instead of the full amount of $150,000.

Variable costing more accurately represent the costs incurred by the company because all $150,000 is expensed.

Now absorption costing resulted in a lower net income because production is less than units sold. Under absorption, a higher fixed MOH was incurred, resulting in the lower net income.

There is a clear pattern between the relationship between product and sales and the resulting net income under the two costing systems:

Production & Sales Resulting Net Income
Production > Sales Absorption > Variable
Production < Sales Absorption < Variable

What if production varies but sales are constant?

If sales are constant, then logicaly net income should not differ. Under absorption costing however, net income does differ.

This is because of how unit product costs are determined under absorption costing. The fixed MOH portion is calculated by:

Total fixed MOH / # of units produced

As a result, a change in number of units produced will result in a change in the per unit product cost.
